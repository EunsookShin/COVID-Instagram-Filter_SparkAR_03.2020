Covid Buying Spree

How Americans’ Pandemic Shopping Behavior Changed Sales Figures



Click to try!


by Eileen Chen, Jessica Hadiwijoyo, Justin Ly, Victoria Shin


Crowded grocery shops and empty commodity shelves became the new normal in the beginning of the pandemic as people panic filled their pantries with everyday necessities which they thought would protect them from the novel coronavirus.

While toilet paper and hand sanitizers seem the intuitive most shopped item when the question comes to mind, aerosol disinfectants, thermometer and oat milk were the actual top three whose sales increased the most in the U.S. during the week ending March 14 compared to that in 2019, with a 519% increase in the sales of aerosol disinfectants, 498% in thermometers and 477% in oat milk.

Inspired by a data story published by the Time magazine, the JOVRNALISM team created an Instagram AR filter that presents the change in U.S. sales figures of eight common goods through an interactive quiz.

“We used 3D models and proportional scaling animation to make the data more interactive,” said Victoria Shin, one of the four members of the team. “We decided to make it in a quiz format so users can use it more interactively. We also decided to display the models on the back camera, so it has more AR touch to it.”

In this AR filter, users are being asked “Which item had a higher increase in sales after COVID-19?” and to choose between a randomly generated pair of items among the total eight entries, including toilet paper, hand sanitizer, bottle water, sunscreen, oat milk, perfume, aerosol disinfectant and thermometer.

After users make the selection by tilting their heads, they switch the camera and check their answers as the AR models launch, visualizing the increase and decrease in sales through projected arrows and percentages, as well as the changes in model sizes.

According to Jessica Hadiwijoyo, who also worked on this project, the team first came up with a storyboard of what they envisioned the filter to be like, and collected feedback from the rest of the JOVRNALISM class to build a rough draft. After the usability test, they further adjusted the data presentation by addressing users’ specific struggles.

“For example, we really wanted to do the visualizing increase and decrease in sales using the scaling up or scaling down the model, because we felt like it was the least distracting as opposed to having small particles of the object fly around in increasing or decreasing amount,” Hadiwijoyo said.

The team also decided that flipping the camera to launch the AR models was an essential feature, for it is easier for the user to see the sales difference by comparing it to their direct surroundings.

“It's more intuitive to switch your back camera and sort of pan around your surroundings than to do it on selfie camera and try to move the camera around,” Hadiwijoyo said.

Designed in a user-friendly way, the filter is both informative and entertaining, adding to people’s existing knowledge of the pandemic. “With all the hoarding going on in the news about toilet paper, hand sanitizers and alcohol, this project is taking this piece of interesting data of what people are buying,” Hadiwijoyo said. “I think seeing that [what the inside of grocery stores looked like] in person makes you curious, and this project helps you address that.”
